This site's title refers to Ted Williams' feat of attaining a .406 batting average for the Boston Red Sox in 1941. For the uneducated, a batting average signifies how often a player is expected to hit safely during an at-bat.

Hitting over .400 is an extremely rare feat. In fact, Williams' average in 1941 was the last time a Major League player has hit that mark.

Focus, Consistency & Confidence

The baseball season is over 160 games long. Games are physically and mentally tiring; often lasting over 4 hours. To maintain extreme focus for so many games over the course of the season without succumbing to injury or mental fatigue is near impossible.

Most of life is the same. The entrepreneur must meet every challenge with an exceptional amount of vigor. The successful entrepreneur must out-think, out-train and out-perform the competition. They must do this everyday, without fail. When they do fail, which they inevitably will, they must pick themselves up, dust themselves off, learn from their failures and continue to move forward.

Before the last game of 1941, Ted Williams batting average stood at .39955. According to the league statisticians, his average would be rounded up and he was guaranteed to record a .400 average without playing the final game. Williams, however, decided to play as he didn't feel he deserved to be recognized for his accomplishment if he sat out the final game. He ended up batting 6-for-8 for the day, and finished the season with an average of .406
